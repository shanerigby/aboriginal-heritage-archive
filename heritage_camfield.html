Wave Hill & Camfield Station

Victoria River District, Northern Territory

Traditional Custodians: The Gurindji, Mudburra, and Warlpiri Peoples

About the People

These distinct but deeply interconnected groups share custody over the vast Victoria River District. The Gurindji and Mudburra people are historically intertwined, but all three groups share international recognition for the 1966 Wave Hill Walk-off—a monumental strike for fair wages and land rights that forever changed the trajectory of Indigenous recognition in Australia.

The vast pastoral leases of the Victoria River District hold deep significance in both Indigenous history and archaeology. Wave Hill Station is internationally renowned as the site of the 1966 Wave Hill Walk-Off. In August of that year, approximately 200 Gurindji, Mudburra, and Warlpiri stockmen and domestic workers walked off the station in protest against poor wages and lack of land rights. Led by Vincent Lingiari, this strike lasted nine years and became a landmark catalyst for the Aboriginal Land Rights (Northern Territory) Act 1976.

Nearby Camfield Station is notable for its ancient archaeological heritage. The region contains highly significant evidence of early occupation, including ancient rock art. Research in the area has documented specific sites featuring detached blocks bearing complex non-figurative motif engravings, such as concentric circles, ellipses, and meandering lines that hold deep cultural resonance for the Traditional Owners.

A Note on Artefact Protection

Under strict local and federal laws, all Aboriginal archaeological places and objects are now definitively protected. Visitors encountering unknown potential artefacts in the wild must invariably leave them undisturbed and report them to relevant Traditional Owner groups or State Heritage Branches.
