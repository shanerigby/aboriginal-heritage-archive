Traditional Custodians: The Ngarabul (Ngoorabul) People

About the People

The Ngarabul (also spelled Ngoorabul) people are the traditional owners and custodians of the Glen Innes area. The Ngarabul name for the town of Glen Innes is Gindaaydjin, which translates to "plenty of big round stones on clear plains" - a direct reference to the prominent granite boulders that define the local highland landscape. They have lived in and adapted to the highland climate of the region for thousands of years.

The history of the area is rich but highly complex. During the colonial era, the area was fundamentally impacted by aggressive pastoral leasing and mining operations, which severely disrupted traditional Indigenous lifestyles. However, descendants of the Ngarabul people - supported by organizations like the Glen Innes Local Aboriginal Land Council - continue to maintain a deep connection to their culture and traditional way of life today. The Aboriginal breastplate found discarded in a historic rubbish tip in the town serves as a poignant physical reminder of this complex transitional era in New South Wales colonial history.

A Note on Artefact Protection

Under strict local and federal laws (such as the NSW National Parks and Wildlife Act 1974 and the Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act), all Aboriginal archaeological places and objects are definitively protected. Visitors encountering potential artefacts must invariably leave them undisturbed and report them to relevant Traditional Owner groups or State Heritage Branches.
