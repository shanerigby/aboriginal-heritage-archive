Traditional Custodians: The Kabi Kabi (Gubbi Gubbi) People

About the People

The Kabi Kabi (or Gubbi Gubbi) people hold deep ancestral ties to the coastline and lush hinterlands of South-East Queensland. Historically, they managed a landscape of immense biodiversity and are deeply celebrated for the cultural tradition of resolving disputes and forging alliances during the great Bunya nut festivals in the nearby Blackall Range.

The Sunshine Coast hinterland is the traditional Country of the Kabi Kabi (also known as Gubbi Gubbi) people. The stunning peaks of the Glass House Mountains and the surrounding forested valleys contain deeply sacred and ceremonial sites. Kabi Kabi physical heritage is documented through local bora rings, scarred trees, and prominent axe-grinding grooves found along ancient riverbeds.

Despite severe systemic displacement during early European settlement, the Kabi Kabi people maintained their unyielding connection to the landscape, which culminated in a historic Federal Court determination in June 2024 that formally recognized them as Native Title holders over expansive portions of the Sunshine Coast.

A Note on Artefact Protection

Under strict local and federal laws (such as the Queensland Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2003), all Aboriginal archaeological places and objects are now definitively protected. Visitors encountering unknown potential artefacts in the wild must invariably leave them undisturbed and report them to relevant Traditional Owner groups or State Heritage Branches.
