Aboriginal Artifacts

by Esma and Jim Rigby

On our travels around Australia between 1990 and 2010 we collected agates from Agate Creek in northwest Queensland. As we moved westwards we collected semiprecious stones, as well as other colourful rocks that were worth polishing.

During our travels, we would occasionally encounter displaced artifacts washed down into creek beds following heavy seasonal flooding, completely separated from their original context.

The artifacts that we kept were ones that were of special interest to us and were almost all from the Agate Creek area. A favourite area for thunder eggs was on the northwest edge of the fossicking reserve where Jim did most of the digging in shallow holes to find the "eggs" that we were looking for.

Sometimes I wandered around at the top of the hill looking for more thunder egg possibilities and came upon a water hole which had water in it and looked quite deep. Above that there were shelves on the hillside and looked interesting but I had been away from Jim for so long that I knew that he would have been ready to go back to camp to have lunch.

On our next climb to the thunder egg site was two years later and I decided to investigate the water hole that I had visited on the previous trip. The water hole was dry and partially filled with sand. I investigated the rock shelving and found that there were aboriginal artifacts scattered all around the site. It seemed to me that this had been a place where the men gathered to make their tools for scraping skins and making sharp pointed implements for, maybe, spears.

On a third trip around Australia in 2007, we called in at Agate Creek and then continued westwards until we came to Camfield Station, a vast pastoral lease in the Northern Territory's Victoria River Downs station owned by the Australian Agricultural Company.

We, and another couple, had visited this station on two previous occasions and had been given permission by the managers to camp near Windmill Tank waterhole. However, on our third visit the new manager refused us permission there but told us that we could camp opposite their gate further west in an area which had been a gravel dump for road making. This was very close to a favourite spot for collecting amethyst crystals.

Fossicking! We crossed the road to open the gate, and it was here we serendipitously noticed a small spear point resting on the surface. Recognizing the fragility of its location so close to a roadway and an active gravel dump, it was carefully collected to ensure it was not crushed by passing station machinery or permanently lost to the elements.

So this is how we became the owners of a small collection of Aboriginal artifacts which we thought needed to be repatriated, hence the origin of this project.

Esma Rigby
